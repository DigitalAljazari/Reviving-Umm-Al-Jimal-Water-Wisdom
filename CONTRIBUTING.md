# Contributing to Reviving Umm Al-Jimal's Water Wisdom

Thank you for your interest in contributing to **Reviving Umm Al-Jimal's Water Wisdom**.

This interdisciplinary initiative brings together water heritage, hydrology, archaeology, artificial intelligence, digital simulation, ecosystem restoration, education, community participation, and peacebuilding.

We welcome responsible contributions from researchers, engineers, archaeologists, ecologists, educators, students, local knowledge holders, community members, civil-society organizations, public institutions, technology specialists, and other qualified contributors.

Contributions should strengthen scientific quality, public value, community benefit, safety, transparency, and respect for heritage and local knowledge.

## 2. Contribution Principles

All contributions should follow these principles:

- **Accuracy:** Claims should be supported by evidence or clearly identified as proposals.
- **Transparency:** Assumptions, limitations, uncertainties, and sources should be stated.
- **Safety:** No contribution should encourage unsafe water infrastructure or unverified construction.
- **Heritage respect:** Archaeological sites and historic structures must be treated responsibly.
- **Community dignity:** Local people should be represented respectfully and accurately.
- **Environmental responsibility:** Proposed interventions must consider ecological consequences.
- **Attribution:** Sources and contributors must receive appropriate acknowledgment.
- **No misrepresentation:** Proposed, simulated, tested, and completed activities must remain clearly distinguished.
- **Non-exclusivity:** Contribution does not create exclusive control over the project's identity or future partnerships.
- **Public value:** Contributions should serve research, education, resilience, responsible innovation, and community benefit.

## 4. How to Propose a Contribution

### Option A: Open an Issue

Use a GitHub Issue to:

- Suggest a research source.
- Identify an error.
- Propose a technical improvement.
- Recommend a partnership area.
- Ask a scientific or methodological question.
- Suggest a new document, diagram, dataset, or tool.
- Report a broken link or formatting problem.

When opening an Issue, include:

- A clear title.
- A short description of the proposal or problem.
- Why it matters to the project.
- Supporting evidence or references.
- Any risks, limitations, or uncertainties.
- Your proposed next step.

Do not place private, sensitive, or confidential information in a public Issue.

### Option B: Submit a Pull Request

A Pull Request may be used to propose:

- Corrections to existing documents.
- New research references.
- Improved explanations.
- Translation or accessibility improvements.
- New documentation.
- Code, modelling, or visualization contributions.
- Approved images, diagrams, or educational materials.

A Pull Request should:

1. Address one clear topic.
2. Explain what was changed.
3. Explain why the change improves the project.
4. Identify sources and evidence.
5. Disclose any limitations or conflicts of interest.
6. Avoid unrelated modifications.
7. Respect the repository's licensing and attribution requirements.

Submitting a Pull Request does not guarantee acceptance.

### Option C: Contact the Project

Contact the project directly when a contribution involves:

- A formal research partnership.
- Engineering or technical services.
- Funding or equipment.
- Confidential information.
- Sensitive maps or land information.
- Identifiable community participants.
- Unpublished research.
- Intellectual property.
- Commercial use.
- Institutional agreements.
- Governmental or regulatory coordination.

**Email:** contact@digitalaljazari.org

6. Research and Citation Standards

Research contributions should:

Prefer peer-reviewed research, official data, recognized technical standards, and authoritative heritage sources.
Provide complete and verifiable references.
Distinguish primary sources from secondary interpretation.
Indicate the geographical and climatic context of evidence.
Avoid assuming that a solution effective elsewhere will work at Deir Al-Kahf.
Identify uncertainty, conflicting evidence, and data gaps.
Avoid fabricated, unverifiable, or AI-invented citations.
Use quotations responsibly and within copyright limitations.

A recommended reference should include:

Author or organization.
Title.
Year.
Publication or publisher.
DOI or stable link where available.
A brief explanation of its relevance.
7. Technical and Engineering Contributions

Technical proposals must clearly state whether they are:

A preliminary idea.
A conceptual design.
A simulated scenario.
An expert recommendation.
A technically reviewed design.
A field-tested intervention.
A verified result.

Technical contributions should address:

Site-specific conditions.
Rainfall and runoff assumptions.
Soil and infiltration characteristics.
Storage calculations.
Sediment management.
Overflow and drainage.
Evaporation.
Water quality.
Human and animal safety.
Inspection and maintenance.
Environmental effects.
Applicable approvals and standards.

No repository contribution should be treated as construction authorization.

Physical implementation requires qualified engineering review, environmental and social safeguards, community consultation, and all necessary legal approvals.

8. Water-Safety Principles

Contributions involving physical water infrastructure must respect the project's safety principles:

No large, deep, unprotected open-water structures.
Preference for covered primary storage.
Limited exposed water surfaces.
Safe access for inspection and maintenance.
Protection of children, visitors, livestock, and wildlife.
Controlled sediment management.
Safe overflow pathways.
Efficient water distribution.
Appropriate water-quality safeguards.
Clear operating and maintenance responsibilities.

The project's current conceptual sequence is:

Runoff channels → protected sedimentation basin → covered main cistern or tank → drip irrigation network → recharge and infiltration garden

Any proposed modification must explain its technical, environmental, and safety implications.

9. Ecological Contributions

Ecological proposals should:

Prioritize locally appropriate native species.
Avoid invasive or water-intensive plants.
Consider soil, rainfall, grazing, salinity, and maintenance.
Explain the source of species recommendations.
Distinguish traditional use from verified ecological suitability.
Address plant survival and long-term water demand.
Consider biodiversity and habitat effects.
Avoid unsupported restoration claims.

Candidate species must not be planted solely because they are mentioned in project documentation. Final selection requires site-specific ecological assessment.

10. Community and Local Knowledge

Contributions involving community or traditional knowledge should:

Respect knowledge holders.
Obtain appropriate consent.
Provide fair acknowledgment.
Preserve cultural and environmental context.
Avoid extracting knowledge for unrelated commercial use.
Consider benefit-sharing where appropriate.
Protect confidential or sensitive knowledge.
Avoid speaking on behalf of communities without authorization.

Women, young people, farmers, pastoral communities, educators, and local residents should be treated as knowledge partners rather than only as beneficiaries.

11. Images and Field Documentation

Before contributing an image, confirm that:

You created the image or have permission to submit it.
Identifiable individuals have provided appropriate consent.
The caption is accurate.
The date and general context are recorded where appropriate.
The image does not expose sensitive archaeological or personal information.
The image has not been misleadingly edited.
Ownership and reuse conditions are clearly stated.

Do not submit:

Images copied from the internet without permission.
Misleading AI-generated images presented as field evidence.
Images exposing private documents or precise sensitive locations.
Photographs that undermine the dignity or safety of participants.
12. Artificial Intelligence Use

AI tools may support research organization, modelling, translation, visualization, drafting, and education, but their use must be transparent and responsible.

Contributors using AI should:

Verify facts, calculations, and citations.
Disclose material AI assistance where relevant.
Never present AI-generated information as verified field evidence.
Avoid fabricated references.
Protect confidential and personal information.
Document important model assumptions.
Keep human experts responsible for technical and scientific decisions.
Distinguish simulation from observation.
Respect copyright and data rights.

AI output does not replace archaeological, hydrological, ecological, engineering, legal, or community expertise.

13. Data Contributions

Data contributions should include:

A clear title and description.
Source and ownership.
Collection method.
Date or period covered.
Geographic scope.
Units and variable definitions.
Known limitations.
Missing-data explanation.
Quality-control procedures.
Permitted uses.
Privacy and sensitivity assessment.

Do not publish precise personal, land, archaeological, or security-sensitive data without review and authorization.

14. Languages and Accessibility

Contributions in Arabic and English are welcome.

Translations should:

Preserve the meaning of the original.
Use consistent project terminology.
Avoid adding unsupported claims.
Identify the translator where appropriate.
Be reviewed for technical accuracy.
Maintain consistency between diagrams, captions, and accompanying text.

Contributors are encouraged to use clear language, descriptive headings, image captions, and accessible explanations.

15. Attribution and Contributor Recognition

Accepted contributors may be acknowledged through:

GitHub contribution history.
Document acknowledgments.
Named authorship where appropriate.
Technical-review recognition.
Research-paper authorship according to accepted academic standards.
Partnership or advisory listings subject to agreement.

Authorship is not granted automatically. It should reflect substantial intellectual or technical contribution and follow recognized academic and ethical standards.

Submitting a contribution does not create employment, ownership, institutional representation, exclusive partnership, or authority to speak on behalf of the project.

16. Rights and Permissions

By submitting material, contributors confirm that:

They created the material or have authority to submit it.
The contribution does not knowingly infringe copyright, privacy, confidentiality, or other rights.
Sources and third-party materials are properly identified.
Required consent has been obtained.
They allow the project to review, edit, publish, preserve, and display the accepted contribution within the repository and related project documentation, with appropriate attribution.

Separate written agreements may be required for major datasets, software, technical designs, commissioned work, confidential material, commercial arrangements, or jointly owned intellectual property.

See LICENSE.md for the repository's use and licensing terms.

17. Review Process

Contributions may be reviewed for:

Relevance.
Accuracy.
Evidence quality.
Safety.
Environmental responsibility.
Heritage protection.
Community dignity.
Legal and ethical risk.
Clarity and accessibility.
Compatibility with the project roadmap.
Licensing and permission requirements.

The project may:

Accept the contribution.
Request revisions.
Seek expert review.
Postpone consideration.
Decline the contribution.
Remove material later if legal, ethical, scientific, or safety concerns emerge.

A declined contribution does not necessarily imply that the idea lacks value; it may be outside the project's current scope, evidence level, resources, or development stage.

18. Respectful Participation

Contributors must communicate respectfully.

The following are not acceptable:

Harassment or discrimination.
Personal attacks.
Deliberate misinformation.
Plagiarism.
False claims of partnership or authority.
Exploitation of community knowledge.
Unsafe technical instructions.
Unauthorized commercial promotion.
Publication of private or sensitive information.
Attempts to appropriate the project's identity or other contributors' work.

Serious or repeated violations may result in rejection or restriction of participation.
