# Reviving Umm Al-Jimal's Water Wisdom

## Heritage-Inspired Water Innovation for Arid Regions

**Reviving Umm Al-Jimal's Water Wisdom** is an interdisciplinary research and innovation project that transforms the historic rainwater-harvesting intelligence of Umm Al-Jimal, Jordan, into a contemporary model for water security, climate resilience, ecosystem recovery, education, and peacebuilding in arid and water-stressed regions.

The project begins from a simple but powerful idea: communities that lived for centuries in dry basalt landscapes developed practical systems for collecting, slowing, filtering, storing, and distributing scarce rainfall. This knowledge should not remain only as archaeological memory. It can be studied, digitally reconstructed, scientifically evaluated, and responsibly adapted to help address present and future water challenges.

The initiative combines heritage research, field observation, hydrological thinking, artificial intelligence, digital simulation, community participation, and safe low-cost water infrastructure. Its first replication pathway is being developed for Jordan's Northern Badia, with a pilot site at Deir Al-Kahf.

---

## The Challenge

Arid and semi-arid communities face interconnected pressures:

- Low and highly variable rainfall.
- Rapid runoff and the loss of rainwater before it can be used or absorbed.
- High evaporation rates.
- Land degradation and declining native vegetation.
- Increasing climate uncertainty.
- Pressure on farming, livelihoods, and local water supplies.
- Unequal access to water and the social tensions that scarcity may intensify.
- A growing separation between historic environmental knowledge and modern education.

Many current responses treat these problems independently. This project addresses them as one connected system in which heritage, technology, water, vegetation, education, and social stability reinforce one another.

## Why Umm Al-Jimal?

Umm Al-Jimal lies within Jordan's basalt desert, where communities historically survived under severe water constraints. The site preserves evidence of an integrated water culture that used stone-built channels, collection surfaces, sediment control, reservoirs, cisterns, gravity, overflow pathways, and careful storage practices.

The importance of Umm Al-Jimal is not limited to the individual structures. Its deeper value lies in the logic connecting them:

1. Observe where rain falls and how runoff moves.
2. Guide water through suitable channels.
3. Slow the flow and allow sediment to settle.
4. Store water safely while limiting evaporation and contamination.
5. Distribute water according to need and terrain.
6. Direct overflow toward soil, vegetation, and productive landscapes.

This systems-based intelligence offers a valuable foundation for designing context-sensitive solutions for today's drylands.

## Project Vision

The project envisions a future in which ancient water wisdom and modern science work together to help dryland communities become more resilient, productive, environmentally secure, and peaceful.

Rather than copying archaeological structures literally, the project studies the principles behind them and redesigns those principles for contemporary safety, environmental conditions, community needs, and available technologies.

---

## Core Innovation

The project's innovation is the creation of a bridge between five fields that are often separated:

### 1. Water Heritage

Document and interpret the water-harvesting logic preserved at Umm Al-Jimal, including the relationship between buildings, surfaces, channels, sedimentation, storage, overflow, and surrounding land.

### 2. Artificial Intelligence and Digital Simulation

Develop AI-assisted models and digital simulations that can examine rainfall scenarios, runoff pathways, storage options, evaporation reduction, irrigation demand, and potential vegetation recovery.

### 3. Safe Physical Replication

Translate the historic principles into a modern, safe, and maintainable water-harvesting sequence:

**Runoff channels -> protected sedimentation basin -> covered main cistern or tank -> drip irrigation network -> recharge and infiltration garden**

The design avoids large, deep, uncovered ponds. It prioritizes covered storage, limited exposed water surfaces, sediment control, safe access, efficient drip irrigation, and protection for people and animals. Where appropriate, shade structures or solar panels may further reduce heat exposure and evaporation.

### 4. Ecosystem Restoration

Use harvested water efficiently to support the recovery of locally appropriate native and drought-tolerant vegetation. Candidate species are selected only after ecological assessment and consultation with local knowledge holders. The aim is to improve soil stability, biodiversity, microclimate, and landscape resilience without introducing unsuitable or water-intensive planting.

### 5. Community Learning and Peacebuilding

Turn the system into a visible learning platform for students, researchers, families, farmers, women, young people, and local institutions. Water cooperation can strengthen social trust, reduce competition over scarce resources, support livelihoods, and demonstrate how shared environmental challenges can become opportunities for collective action.

## Deir Al-Kahf Pilot Pathway

Deir Al-Kahf in Jordan's Northern Badia has been identified as the first proposed replication landscape. The site reflects many of the conditions the project is designed to address: low rainfall, basalt-rich terrain, scattered vegetation, exposed soil, agricultural surroundings, and the need for water-efficient ecological recovery.

Field visits and preliminary site documentation have begun. The pilot is currently in the assessment and design stage. Before construction, the project will require detailed topographic, hydrological, soil, ecological, engineering, safety, and community assessments.

The proposed pilot will test how the principles learned from Umm Al-Jimal can be adapted to a living contemporary landscape without compromising archaeological integrity or presenting an unverified design as a finished solution.

## Project Objectives

- Preserve and reinterpret the water heritage of Umm Al-Jimal as living knowledge.
- Produce a digital representation of the historic water system and its operating logic.
- Develop AI-assisted tools for rainfall, runoff, storage, irrigation, and vegetation scenarios.
- Design and evaluate a safe water-harvesting pilot for the Northern Badia.
- Reduce evaporation and improve the productive use of harvested rainwater.
- Support native vegetation recovery and land rehabilitation.
- Create practical educational materials for schools, universities, and communities.
- Document local and women's knowledge related to water, plants, land, and household resilience.
- Strengthen dialogue around water as a shared resource and a foundation for peace.
- Produce an adaptable model for other arid communities in Jordan, the Middle East, and comparable drylands worldwide.

## Responsible Development Principles

The project follows several essential principles:

- **Evidence before construction:** field observations are followed by technical verification.
- **Safety by design:** no large, deep, unprotected bodies of water.
- **Low evaporation:** preference for covered cisterns or tanks and minimal exposed surfaces.
- **Ecological suitability:** planting decisions must respect native ecosystems and available water.
- **Heritage respect:** archaeological sites are studied and interpreted, not altered without proper authority.
- **Community participation:** local people are partners in knowledge, design, monitoring, and learning.
- **Transparent claims:** proposed, tested, and completed activities are clearly distinguished.
- **Open learning:** non-sensitive knowledge and tools should be shared whenever possible.
- **Adaptation, not duplication:** each replication must respond to its own terrain, climate, culture, and water conditions.
